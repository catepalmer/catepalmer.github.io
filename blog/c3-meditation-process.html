Meditation Process

How did the 'process over product' concept affect the way you tackled the site redesign and rebuild?

I struggled with this quite a bit - it was difficult not to get caught up in trying to make my blog look perfect, especially given that I lacked the skills to easily fix really glaring problems in the CSS code (and would end up spending a long time trying to figure out how to fix these). Overall I think I could’ve done a lot better with this aspect of my redesign and rebuild, largely through having more of a plan and method for the process itself (as it was tricky having limited time and only being able to work on the redesign in short bursts, but then not remembering what I’d been doing the last time when I finally came back to it). I think in future I’d like to approach this kind of exercise with more of a plan for my process and with quite clear note-taking for tracking this along the way.

What did you think about meditation before reading chapter 2 of Search Inside Yourself?

Before reading chapter 2, I thought of meditation as a relaxation practice that was fairly aimless aside from the relaxation produced by the exercise - I knew it was beneficial in reducing stress levels and promoting wellness, but I didn’t really think of it as a practice with a purpose aside from these side-benefits.

What new things have you learnt about meditation?

I really like the way Tan explains meditations as a means of training the mind, with the goal being the ability to produce and maintain the kind of focus that leads to greater self-awareness and insight, and the way he likens this ‘training’ to weight training (with growth resulting from the overcoming of resistance). This made a lot of sense to me in terms of the tangible aims of meditation and measurable effects it produces.

Did any of the suggested meditation techniques stand out to you? Any other musings?

I liked Tan’s advice to do whatever allows you to feel most calm and relaxed in terms of posture and whether your eyes are open or closed - as someone with a back condition that makes it very hard for me to sit without back support without experiencing pain, usual suggestions for meditation posture don’t work well for me, and I much prefer to lie down - Tan’s ‘Do what works best for you’ seemed to speak directly to this. And I like the idea of starting with an intention, e.g. to reduce stress or increase your own wellbeing - those kinds of intentions seemed to specifically speak to the self-care and self-compassion that are intrinsic parts of meditation. I also liked the way Tan says that because meditation is training (with the implication being that when you start training at something you’re not necessarily going to be very good), and self-compassion is an essential part of meditation, there’s no such thing as a failed meditation - and being harsh with yourself when your mind wanders as you’re trying to meditate is beside the point.
