Daily Mindfulness

What did you learn from your 'mindfulness conversation'? How did the other person find it?

I did this exercise with my mum via Skype, and I found it really interesting as it cemented for me that some of the improvements we’ve seen in our relationship over the past year or so have come about partly because we’ve navigated some conflicts with each other in a way that has seemed to echo Tan’s definitions of listening, looping and dipping, and we’ve generally been more attentive when listening to each other since then in a way that I think has something in common with Tan’s definition of mindfulness (and I feel like I’ve also had more meta-awareness of when I haven’t engaged with her in a very mindful way). I also think what Tan says about these techniques being particularly helpful in navigating conflict rings very true for me, as there have been a couple of other major conflicts I’ve had with people in the past year that have resulted in the opposite of a closer or better relationship, and in one case in particular the absence of things like looping and dipping (or their equivalent) on the other person’s part was largely what made me decide the conflict was unnavigable and I needed to distance myself from that person, as I simply didn’t feel like I was being listened to. So I think the techniques Tan talks about, and the general concept of mindful engagement with others, has wider implications for the health of relationships and their ability to bounce back from conflict. I think my mum found the exercise amusing but helpful, and for her it also seemed to echo some things we’ve been trying to do more with each other lately.

Which of the three suggested techniques will you use to sustain your practice?

I really like the idea of doing less than you can, and Tan’s focus on making a practice sustainable and not something that feels like a burden. This is something I feel like I’ve already grappled with a bit, as I had a tendency when I was younger to not cut myself any slack and to expect too much of myself (and then I’d predictably burn out and be unable to sustain a routine with what I was trying to do).
