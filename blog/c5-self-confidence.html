Self-Confidence

What is the relationship between mindfulness and self awareness?

Both mindfulness and self-awareness involve being able to retain an awareness and attention that’s slightly outside of your stream of thought and emotion, so that you’re aware of your emotions and thoughts as experiences that are passing through you, rather than over-identifying with them. Both also involve being able to see thoughts and emotions with clarity. There’s a direct link between mindfulness and self-awareness in that the more you practise mindfulness, the more self-aware you’re likely to become (through being able to apply greater clarity to your emotional experience).

Try at least one of the self awareness practices from the chapter (Body Scan, Journaling). What was your experience of it? How did you feel?

I already have a regular journalling practice (largely for similar reasons to what Tan outlines as its benefits - increased clarity about my thoughts and emotions and ability to work with them to solve problems I’m experiencing), but the technique he suggests is a bit different from what I usually do (in that he advocates a kind of stream-of-consciousness journalling), so I decided to try it. I found it helpful to just get my thoughts and feelings down on paper - almost like doing a messy first draft that allowed me to see the basic material I was working with in terms of what was going on in my mind and what I was feeling fixated on. By itself this gave me a feeling of a slight distance from and objectivity around my thoughts and emotions - which gave me a sense of calm and not being as caught up in or anxious about the things that were running through my mind.

What did you take away from the "My Emotions are Not Me" section?

I found this section really resonated with me and echoed quite a few other ideas I’d encountered in my efforts to develop more self-awareness and mastery over my emotions (which is something I’ve been very interested in for a few years now). I like the idea that developing self-awareness allows you to go from seeing your emotions as existential to seeing them as experiential, and being able to work productively with them rather than being unable to do anything about them. This really resonated with me and made me feel quite excited about the possibilities presented by this way of thinking. I also think it’s a much more accurate way of looking at your emotions than seeing them as essential to who you are. I really liked the metaphor of thoughts and emotions being like clouds, and our core self being like the sky - this was a really comforting thought, as the clear sky is always still there even if it’s hidden temporarily by the clouds, and learning tools that allow you to access it more easily provide a lot of hope for being able to not be overwhelmed by your emotions and thoughts.
